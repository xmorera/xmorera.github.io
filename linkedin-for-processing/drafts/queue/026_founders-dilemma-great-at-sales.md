## Title
The Founder's Dilemma: You're Great at Sales, So You Don't Scale

## Description
The cruelest trap in early-stage startups: the founder is great at selling the product, so they keep doing it. And doing it. And doing it. Until the company's growth ceiling is literally the founder's calendar capacity. This is the story of recognizing this trap — and the uncomfortable transition of letting go of the thing you're best at.

## Key Points
- The trap mechanics: you're the best salesperson because you built the product, know every detail, and care more than anyone else
- The short-term win: you close deals that nobody else would close
- The long-term cost: you become the single point of failure for every deal, every demo, every customer relationship
- The invisible cost: every hour in a sales call is an hour not spent on product, strategy, team building, or partnerships
- The moment it breaks: when your pipeline is full but you physically cannot take more calls — and revenue stalls
- What the transition requires: documentation of the pitch, building a repeatable process, trusting that someone else can close (differently, but effectively)
- The founder ego component: it's hard to trust the pitch to someone else when you know you'd do it better
- The reframe: your job as a founder is not to be the best salesperson — it's to build a company that can sell without you
- What Xavier is working through with Lupo.AI: partners, resellers, Microsoft marketplace as channels that scale the pitch beyond his calendar
- CTA: "How did you break out of the founder-as-salesperson trap? What made the handoff work?"
