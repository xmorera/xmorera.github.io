## Title
Multilingual Training Isn't a Nice-to-Have. It's a Competitive Advantage Most Companies Are Ignoring.

## Description
Companies spend millions deploying software globally, then ship English-only training to teams in Brazil, Mexico, France, and Japan. This is not a small efficiency problem — it's a strategic blind spot. Implementation delays, lower adoption, support overload, and partner churn all trace back to the same root: your partners and employees can't use what they don't understand. This post makes the business case for multilingual training as a core competitive asset.

## Key Points
- The global enterprise reality: 70%+ of enterprise software is used by teams who are not native English speakers
- The typical response: ship English training and hope for the best. Or hire translators (expensive, slow).
- What happens when non-native speakers get English-only training: they consume less, retain less, and need more support
- Real business impact: delayed implementations, higher support costs, lower product adoption, partner attrition
- The Latin America angle: one of the fastest-growing enterprise software markets — yet most software companies treat Spanish-language training as an afterthought
- AI changes the math: professional-quality Spanish, Portuguese, French training no longer costs 10x the English version
- The first-mover advantage: companies that invest in multilingual training now get implementation speed, adoption rates, and partner loyalty their competitors don't have
- Cloudera example (anonymized): competitive advantage cited in deal
- CTA: "How does your company handle training for non-English-speaking users? What's working — or not?"
