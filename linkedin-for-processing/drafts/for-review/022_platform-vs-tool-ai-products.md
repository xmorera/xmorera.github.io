## Title
Platform vs. Tool: Why This Distinction Determines Which AI Products Survive

## Description
In every technology wave, some products remain tools (useful but replaceable) and some become platforms (embedded, sticky, central to how work gets done). The AI wave is no different. This post explores what separates an AI tool from an AI platform and why this distinction is critical for both builders and buyers right now.

## Key Points
- The difference: a tool does a specific task; a platform changes how multiple workflows operate
- The commoditization curve: AI tools are being commoditized rapidly. What costs $50/month today will cost $5 in 18 months as models improve and competition increases.
- Platform survival: a platform survives commoditization because its value comes from integration depth, workflow change, and switching cost — not raw capability
- The buyer's risk: investing in a tool whose core capability gets absorbed by a larger platform (this is already happening with AI writing tools and Microsoft Copilot)
- What makes Lupo.AI a platform argument: it's not just about generating a video — it's about changing how companies transfer knowledge, train partners, and deploy AI agents. That's a workflow change.
- The Innovator's Dilemma angle: the platforms being disrupted (LMS companies, training video studios) have the most to lose and the slowest ability to respond
- For builders: the product question isn't "what can our AI do?" it's "what workflow does it own?"
- For buyers: the evaluation question isn't "can it do X?" it's "will it still exist in 3 years and is it worth our integration investment?"
- CTA: "How do you distinguish between AI tools you're evaluating and ones that could become real platform bets?"
