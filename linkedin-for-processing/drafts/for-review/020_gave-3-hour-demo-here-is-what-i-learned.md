## Title
I Once Gave a 3-Hour Product Demo. Here's the Lesson I Still Carry.

## Description
A story about a demo that went completely wrong — or completely right, depending on how you look at it. When a founder is passionate about their product, the natural impulse is to show everything. The lesson that took years to learn: people don't buy features, they buy the feeling that their specific problem will be solved. The shorter the demo, the faster the deal.

## Key Points
- The scene: an early Lupo.AI demo that turned into a 3-hour deep dive into every feature
- The result: the prospect was impressed. And then they never replied.
- Why: cognitive overload + no clear call to action + the demo replaced the relationship conversation
- The insight about demos and pitches: people make decisions in the first 5-10 minutes, then spend the rest of the time either confirming or looking for reasons to back out
- The "minimal viable demo" principle: show only what solves their stated problem. Nothing else.
- The questions that change demos: "What's the one thing keeping you from solving this today?" then demonstrate only that
- The trust moment: being willing to say "we don't do X" builds more trust than trying to show you do everything
- How this connects to Lupo.AI's pitch evolution: from "here's every feature" to "here's your problem in our platform"
- CTA: "What's the demo lesson you had to learn the hard way?"

## Source Insight
Xavier runs every demo personally and has developed strong intuitions about what works. The "3-hour Zoom problem" in training also mirrors the "3-hour demo problem" in sales — both are about respecting attention and being specific.
