## Title
Your LMS Is a Museum, Not a Learning Platform

## Description
A direct provocation for L&D professionals and HR leaders: most corporate LMS platforms are archives of outdated content that nobody returns to. They were designed for compliance and record-keeping, not for learning. The rise of AI-generated content makes this even more urgent — because now companies can create content at scale, and they'll need a different kind of system to deliver it effectively.

## Key Points
- The LMS promise: centralize your learning, track completion, ensure compliance
- The reality: completion tracking ≠ learning. Most LMS users log in to check a box, not to learn.
- The museum analogy: a museum preserves things. A learning platform accelerates skill development. Most LMS tools are doing the former.
- The content staleness problem: LMS platforms often host training from 3-5 years ago. Nobody flags it as outdated. New employees learn wrong things.
- The push-vs-pull model: traditional LMS is push (here's what you must take). Modern learning is pull (here's what I need right now).
- What AI changes: the ability to generate fresh, contextual content means the bottleneck shifts from production to delivery — and most LMS platforms weren't designed for that
- The "moment of need" principle: the most effective training happens 5 minutes before someone needs to do a thing, not 3 months before in an onboarding module
- What a modern training infrastructure looks like: short content, searchable, role-specific, integrated into workflow tools (Slack, Teams, CRM), measurable by behavior change not just completion
- CTA: "How often do the people in your organization actually go back to your LMS voluntarily — not because they have to?"
